Conventional Current vs. Electron Flow

1. Conventional Current

The Engineering Standard

What it assumes: Current behaves as a flow of positive charge carriers moving from a higher electrical potential to a lower electrical potential.

Higher Voltage (e.g., +5V) → Lower Voltage (Ground / 0V)
  • At the workbench: This flows "downhill" from your positive voltage rails down toward Ground.
  • Where it is used: Almost all circuit schematics, datasheets, industrial documentation, and standard electrical components.
  • Rule of Thumb: Standard symbols (like the arrows on diodes, LEDs, and transistors) point in the direction of conventional current flow.

2. Electron Flow

The Physical Reality

What is actually happening: Negatively charged electrons physically migrate from a lower electrical potential to a higher electrical potential.

Lower Voltage (Ground / 0V) → Higher Voltage (e.g., +5V)
  • At the workbench: Electrons leave your Ground rail (the massive pool of negative charge carriers) and travel up toward the higher positive voltage level.
  • Origin: Discovered in 1897 by J.J. Thomson, establishing that physical charge carriers in metal conductors are negative.
  • Context: Important for solid-state semiconductor design and vacuum tube theory, but ignored for basic circuit layout.
