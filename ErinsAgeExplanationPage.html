How to Read Binary

The COG Lab Official Guide to Tracking Erin's "Age"

COG Lab Context: Every time Erin passes our Harvey Mudd College lab window on her campus tour, we jokingly yell "Happy Birthday!" Naturally, as scientists, we must rigorously keep track of her "age" incrementing by 1 with each tour. Here is how to represent and read that count in binary.

1. The Core Concept: Base-2 vs. Base-10

In our everyday decimal system (Base-10), we count using ten digits (0–9). When we hit 9 and add 1, we roll over to the next "place value" (the tens place). Binary (Base-2) works the exact same way, but it only has two digits: 0 (Off) and 1 (On). Instead of scaling by powers of 10, the column place values scale by powers of 2.

4. Quick COG Lab Cheat Sheet

Next time a tour approaches HMC's COG lab, glance at the board and remember this quick decoding rule: If the rightmost digit is 1, Erin's birthday count is an odd number. If it is 0, her count is even. With just 7 total bits, you can track her up to age 127 before needing to add a 128s column!
